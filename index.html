Optimizing Nitrogen Timing via Calculus \( f'(t) \)

The Bioscience Challenge: Food Security

In Malaysia, maintaining self-sufficiency levels (SSL) for rice is paramount for national food security. The flagship high-yield cultivar, MR219 (developed by MARDI), takes approximately 120 days to mature.

To maximize yields and limit environmental runoff, agronomic scientists must apply top-dressing fertilizers (specifically Nitrogen) at the precise moment of maximum daily biomass accumulation. Applying it too early results in nutrient leaching; applying it too late results in stunted grain filling. We model this biomass curve using calculus to locate this critical day.

The Mathematical Link: The biomass $f(t)$ accumulates logistically. Its instantaneous rate of change is given by the derivative $f'(t)$ (g/hill/day). The day of peak fertilizer demand occurs precisely when the derivative is at its absolute maximum: the inflection point where $f''(t) = 0$.

The Bio-Mathematical Model

Paddy Dry Biomass Accumulation, \( f(t) \)
$$f(t) = \frac{L}{1 + e^{-k(t - t_0)}}$$

Where $L$ is max biomass, $k$ is the relative growth coefficient, $t_0$ is inflection point day, and $t$ is Days After Transplanting (DAT).

Instantaneous Growth Rate, Derivative \( f'(t) \)
$$f'(t) = \frac{L \cdot k \cdot e^{-k(t - t_0)}}{(1 + e^{-k(t - t_0)})^2} = k \cdot f(t) \cdot \left(1 - \frac{f(t)}{L}\right)$$

Derived via the Quotient & Chain Rule. Represents daily dry matter growth velocity in grams per hill per day ($\text{g/hill/day}$).
